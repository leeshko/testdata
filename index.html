Article 1: The Solar System: An Overview The solar system is a gravitationally bound system centered on the Sun, a G-type main-sequence star. It consists of eight major planets, their moons, dwarf planets, asteroids, comets, and countless smaller objects, all orbiting the Sun.

1. The Sun The Sun, a nearly perfect sphere of hot plasma, makes up about 99.86% of the solar system's mass. It provides the energy that sustains life on Earth and drives the system's dynamics. Its surface temperature is approximately 5,500°C, and its core reaches about 15 million°C.

2. The Planets Planets are divided into two categories: Terrestrial Planets: Mercury, Venus, Earth, and Mars. These are rocky and smaller in size. Gas Giants and Ice Giants: Jupiter and Saturn (gas giants) are primarily hydrogen and helium, while Uranus and Neptune (ice giants) contain heavier elements like water, ammonia, and methane.

3. Moons and Satellites Many planets have natural satellites or moons. Earth’s Moon is unique for its size relative to its planet. Jupiter and Saturn host over 80 moons each, including the large and geologically active Io and Europa.

4. Small Solar System Bodies Asteroids: Mostly found in the asteroid belt between Mars and Jupiter. They are rocky remnants from the solar system’s formation. Comets: Icy objects that release gas and dust when they approach the Sun, forming glowing comas and tails. Dwarf Planets: Like Pluto and Ceres, these are too small to clear their orbits of other debris.

5. Beyond the Planets The Kuiper Belt and Oort Cloud lie beyond Neptune. These regions are reservoirs of icy bodies and are thought to be the origin of many comets.

Article 2: Key Features of the Solar System The solar system's structure and dynamics offer a fascinating glimpse into the universe's workings.

1. Formation and Evolution The solar system formed about 4.6 billion years ago from a rotating cloud of gas and dust, called the solar nebula. Gravity caused the nebula to collapse, forming the Sun at its center. Remaining material coalesced into planets, moons, and other bodies.

2. Orbital Dynamics All planets orbit the Sun in the same direction (counterclockwise viewed from above the Sun’s north pole). Most planetary orbits are nearly circular, except for Mercury’s, which is more elliptical. The orbital plane is called the ecliptic plane, though some objects, like Pluto, deviate from it.

3. Unique Features Mercury: Closest to the Sun, with extreme temperature variations. Venus: Earth’s "twin" in size but shrouded in thick clouds of sulfuric acid. Earth: The only planet known to support life, with liquid water covering 70% of its surface. Mars: Known as the "Red Planet" due to its iron oxide-rich soil.

4. The Role of Gravity The Sun’s immense gravity governs the motion of all solar system bodies. Additionally, interactions among planets cause perturbations in orbits, leading to phenomena like orbital resonances.

5. Future of the Solar System The Sun will eventually exhaust its nuclear fuel, becoming a red giant in about 5 billion years. During this phase, it will engulf the inner planets, including Earth, before shedding its outer layers and leaving a white dwarf behind.

Fun Fact The solar system isn’t static; it’s moving through the Milky Way galaxy at an average speed of 828,000 km/h, completing one orbit every 225-250 million years!
